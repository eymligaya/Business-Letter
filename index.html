INTRODUCTION

    Business letters have a significant place in professional communication. Whether you are writing to clients, partners, suppliers, or even employees, the writing form in your letter will give them an impression. A well-written business letter not only shows professionalism but also respect and attention. The report focuses on the elements making a business letter effective. It then follows the widely adopted 7 C's of communication, followed by discussion of the various commonly used formats of business correspondence. These principles ensure that your business letters will be clear, concise, and professional in all respects, with a good impression on the reader.

BUSINESS LETTER

    W. J. Weston said, “Business letter is the process of accomplishing business transactions in written form.” Weston’s shows the relevance and importance of business letters in conducting transactions. Basically, Business Letters is a document in which it is a way to communicate and complete business deals or transactions through writing. Can be used for inquiries, placing orders, or even resolving disputes.

PURPOSE OF BUSINESS LETTER

Convey Information

    The main purpose of any business letter is to convey and share  information regarding business activities. This information can be sent to various people and organizations involved with the business. By using business letters, companies can ensure that important details are clearly and formally conveyed.

Conclude Transaction

    This is one also of the purposes of the Business Letter to conclude in completed or finalize  transactions. A business letter is often used to officially conclude it, this letter can confirm that all necessary details are settled with this both parties have a clear and formal record that the transaction is complete.

Creation of Goodwill

    We are now in advanced technology already, messages can be sent within seconds. However, a well-decorated business letter still holds significant importance in creating a positive image of a company. Goodwill refers to the positive reputation and trust a company earns from its customers and partners in sending thoughtful and well-presented letters. Businesses can show their professionalism, sincerity, and commitment to quality.

Expansion of Business

    Through goodwill messages and through circular letters existing markets can be expanded. Every company has its goal to succeed and by the help of this business letter, business can expand. This can be achieved by sending goodwill messages that have been discussed.

Establishment of Relationship

   Another important purpose of a business letter helps to build and maintain mutual relationships with customers, suppliers, and other interested parties. By communicating clearly, formally, and professionally, business can establish a strong relationship with one another.

Evidence

    One of the purposes of a business letter will serve as documentary evidence that can be used in future because it creates a communication record that can be kept so that in the future if there's a disagreement between parties there is a letter that a business can provide as a proof of what was discussed or agreed upon. By preserving these letters, businesses can protect themselves and ensure that there is a clear and accurate history of their communications.

To Inquire

    Business letter is to ask for information from outside sources. By sending inquiry letters, businesses can request this necessary information. These letters help companies gather the details they need to make informed decisions, solve problems, or explore new opportunities.

Problem Solving

    Business Letters are important in resolving matters knowing that In the business world, misunderstandings and disputes can happen. A business letter helps in resolving a problem in a formal way to address and clarify any disagreements or confusion between parties.

QUALITIES OF EFFECTIVE BUSINESS LETTERS

    One of the keys to effective communication is the quality of a business letter. It not only tells how well your message would be communicated but also ensures that the recipient perceives the sender in just the right way. The 7 C's of communication form a framework which could have helped you be sure that your letters were up to the high standards expected in professional contexts.

Clear:

    Clarity is the most characteristic feature of a business letter. The words used should be simple and uncomplicated so that there is no misunderstanding on the part of the reader as to what he is being told. Avoid the use of difficult words, jargon, or too many technical terms. Thus, whereas if you say "Please find attached the aforementioned documentation for your perusal," you would do well to put it better as, "I attached documents for you to review."

Concise:

    A brief letter is short and the point. In business life, time is valuable, and lengthened information can be a burden to the one receiving the letter. One simply does not want to take away the essence of the message through filling up lines with unwanted information. Get straight to the point and provide the recipient only with the information that is necessary. Good practice is previewing the draft, then eliminating words and phrases that don't add value to communication.

Concrete:

    A concrete message is direct, detailed with no ambiguous space for interpretation. Information conveyed is concrete, and the examples are lucid and transparent. The reader gets

to know the entire content. This can be depicted instead of a sentence like, "The project will be completed soon," a concrete sentence is, "The project will be completed by October 15, 2024." Hence, it gives the reader something clear workable and, at the same time, clears the ambiguity.

Correct:

     It should be grammatically, punctually, and spell correct. A business letter must not carry any grammatical, punctuation, or spelling errors. The sentences should also be correctly structured and must be up to the standards of standard English. Above everything, the data to be carried in the letter should be factually correct. Providing wrong data or carrying the wrong address would undermine your credibility and the overall professionalism of the letter.

Coherent:

    Coherence involves the logical flow of a message. The points should be clear and structured, hence easily understandable by the reader. The transition words like "therefore" and "additionally" keep it flowing in a straight line. A coherent letter, therefore, not only enhances readability but also enables the receiver to absorb the message and not get confused from disjointed ideas.

Courteous:

    A polite tone shows that a writer is considerate of a receiver's emotions. It is a gift in writing a letter since one can muster the skills that will be helpful in avoiding offending the reader. This shows respect for the person's feelings and makes them comfortable with the message or advice given in the letter.

Complete:

    Language used in courteous manners helps establish a good relationship and tone. A letter that is more negative or regretful could still be delivered kindly and politely since kind words such as "please" or "thank you" are very powerful for delivering the message. For instance, instead of "You should correct this now," the right thing to say would be, "We kindly request you address this immediately.

    Complete business letters provide all the information the receiver might need to know. They answer most questions a reader might have so that the receiver will not need to correspond again. Full letters have attachments and enclosures that are relevant to the letter and include clear instruction on what needs to be done next.

PARTS OF BUSINESS LETTER ( ESSENTIAL and OPTIONAL)

ESSENTIAL PARTS OF A BUSINESS LETTER

    The content is important, but there is also the format that comprises a business letter. The paper should ensure there is a standard lay out, hence making it look nice and easy to read. Below are the essential elements of a business letter regardless of the format used:

Heading:

    The heading has the sender's name, address, phone number, and email. This also includes the date. It is on the same line as the sender's details or on a new line below them. This heading introduces the individual writing the letter and gives the recipient any necessary contact details. It is usually left aligned to the top margin of the page.

Recipient Address:

    Put the address of the recipient below the title. To the left margin, put this. Write the full name with the title and their full mailing address. The nomenclature and title must be correctly spelled otherwise the impression will be wrong or the letter misplaced.

Salutation:

    This is a salutation or greeting of the business letter. The most common salutation is "Dear [Title] [Last Name]," as follows: "Dear Mr. Johnson." If you do not know the name of the recipient, "Dear Sir or Madam" is an acceptable alternative. The salutation must always be respectful and professional in tone in respect of the subsequent content of the letter.

Body:

    The body refers to the main part of the letter and is usually divided into three parts:

  • Introduction: This is the first paragraph introducing the reason for the letter. It should be concise and clarify the main reason for writing the letter.
  • Main Content: This part provides the details of the message. It could include facts, explanations, or a request-to-name a few kinds of purposes of writing such a letter.
  • Summary: The final paragraph is a summary, and may even include a call to action or a thank you for the reader's time and attention.

Closing:

     Closing with a polite closing in the letter. Closings are either "Sincerely," "Best regards," or "Thank you." Always capitalize the first letter of the closing, and have a comma after the closing word. This adds the final touch of professionalism at the end of the letter.

Signature:

    Leave blank space above for the sender's signature. If this is a printed letter, there must be enough space to permit the sender to sign their name, in handwriting, over their typed name and title.

    To write an effective business letter, both content and format need attention. The 7 C's–clear, concise, concrete, correct, coherent, courteous, and complete–speak to essential concerns that have to be made by the letter writer in order to ensure that the message meant to be passed across is done professionally and efficiently. Keeping in mind these general guidelines, combined with an understanding of a standard business letter format, should enable you to write clear, effective letters that will create a lasting, positive impression on the recipient. Whether the format you choose is full block, modified block, or semi-block, the key to success is clarity, professionalism, and respect for the reader's time and needs.

OPTIONAL PARTS OF A BUSINESS LETTER

Reference Line:

    This part includes a reference number that helps link the letter to previous or future correspondence on the same topic. Even though it’s optional, many organizations use it to make things easier for both senders and receivers.

Attention Line:

    If a letter is sent to a company but meant for a specific person, their name is mentioned separately in the Attention Line. This line is usually centered on the page and placed after the inside address.

Subject Line:

    The subject line highlights the main idea of the letter. It is usually typed in capital or bold letters at the left margin, after the salutation. Some organizations prefer to place it after the inside address. The subject line helps the reader quickly understand the purpose of the letter

Identification Line:

    Identification Line comprises the initials of names of the persons who are involved in drafting and typing of the message.

Enclosure Line:

    This part of the letter is used when you are including additional documents along with the letter. These could be things like another letter, a report.

Carbon Copies:

    Sometimes copies of a particular letter are the same as some other person or persons. If so, a record of it is kept by mentioning names and addresses of these persons. The abbreviation used for this purpose is 'cc.'"bcc' is used to send a copy to a third person without bringing it into the knowledge of the original Addressee.

PHYSICAL  FEATURES/APPEARANCE

    There are certain standards for formatting a business letter, though some variations are acceptable (for example between European and North American business letters). Here are some basic guidelines:

  • Use A4 (European) or 8.5 x 11 inch (North American) paper or letterhead
  • Use 2.5 cm or 1 inch margins on all four sides
  • Use a simple font such as Times New Roman or Arial
  • Use 10 to 12 point font
  • Use a comma after the salutation (Dear Mr Bond,)
  • Fold in three (horizontally) before placing in the envelope

TYPES OF BUSINESS LETTERS

A. Letter of Inquiry / Reply Letter

Letter of Inquiry

    Inquiry letters are written for the purpose of asking for something from the recipient. Inquiries can be sent as a formal business letter (outside of your company) or as an e-mail. Before sending your inquiry, you should be certain that the information is not available through other means, such as the company website.

There are three primary categories of inquiry letters:

  • Status inquiries are typically sent to request references or recommendations.
  • Routine inquiries are letters written to an individual or company for the purpose of gathering specific information.
  • Sales-related inquiries are written to a company or individual to obtain information about a product or service and may be the first step of collecting information for a report.

Reply Letter

    A letter of reply or response letter is written when one responds to a letter of inquiry. It directly answers all the inquiries regarding the company’s products or services. In writing this letter, you have to express your appreciation for the interest shown by the sender, and state cheerfully the information requested.

Take note of the following when writing this letter:

  1. Acknowledge the inquiry by mentioning important details from the letter of inquiry you received.
  2. Build goodwill and pave the way for future contacts by using a cordial or friendly tone.
  3. Answer the questions fully and send prompt replies.

B. Letter of Inquiry (Sales Possibility) / Reply Letter

Letter of Inquiry (Sales Possibility)

    A sales inquiry letter is the letter that is written to the sales department of the company by the employer, accounts department, etc. The main aim of this letter is to get information about the sales of the company for a particular period, order, sales job, sales procedure, etc. This kind of a letter is written within a company between two different departments to keep track of the conservation and avoid confusion in the future.

    The sales inquiry letter is a formal letter, even though it is written within the company,  thus ensuring that you maintain the format and keep the tone of the letter formal. Make sure that you mention all the details that you need from the sales department about the product, service, job vacancy, etc. The letter should be neither too long nor too short.

Here are the things that should be remembered in writing a sales inquiry letter:

  1. The letter should be formal.
  2. The letter should have no spelling or grammatical mistakes.
  3. The letter should mention the reason for writing this letter.
  4. The letter should be precise.

C. Claim Letter (Complaint Letter) / Letter of Acknowledgement

Claim Letter

    A claim letter is a persuasive letter sent by a customer to a business or agency to identify a problem with a product or service and can also be referred to as a letter of complaint.

    Typically, a claim letter opens (and sometimes closes) with a request for adjustments, such as a refund, replacement, or payment for damages, though a cordial opening paragraph about the transaction or product may be preferred.

    As a method of business writing, claim letters are sent as a legally binding form of communication which can serve as evidence if a claim is taken to court. In most cases, court appearances are not required because the business recipient typically drafts a reply in the form of an adjustment letter, which settles the claim.

    Most business professionals and scholars agree that a basic claim letter should include four core elements: a clear explanation of the complaint, an explanation of what strife this has caused or the losses suffered because of it, an appeal to honesty and fairness, and a statement of what you would consider a fair adjustment in return.

    Preciseness in the explanation is pivotal to the claim being settled quickly and effectively, so a claim writer should provide as much detail about the defectiveness of a product or the fault in service received, including date and time, the amount is cost and receipt or order number, and any other detail that help define exactly what went wrong.

    The inconvenience this fault has caused and an appeal to the reader's humanity and compassion are equally important in getting what the writer wants out of the claim. This provides the reader motivation to act on the writer's request promptly in order to rectify the situation and maintain the customer as a client.

Letter of Acknowledgement 

    The purpose of letters of acknowledgment is to provide proof that you have received specific documents or a specific type of request. Letters of acknowledgment are often used for anything involved in a legal process.

D. Letter of Order

    An order letter is a formal document that approves the purchase and sale of a product and provides product specifications. It may also comprise order characteristics, product specifications, quantity, color, product, mode of payment, and shipment/delivery date(s), among other things. Purchase orders, or POs, are another term for order letters.

    The letter is written in formal language. You must take special care to provide complete and accurate information, as missing information results in delayed deliveries.

Here are the things that should be remembered in writing a letter of order:

  1. The letter should be precise; no pep talk or informal conversation is required.
  2. Your objectives must be properly understood and articulated.
  3. The language should be simple and formal.
  4. Make no errors in the specs or numbers.
  5. Inform the seller when you expect delivery and how you intend to pay the fee.
  6. Jargon should be avoided.
  7. Don’t be afraid to place your order, but remember to plan ahead of time.
  8. If you have any questions, please do not hesitate to contact the receiver.
  9. Instead of a simple A4 page, order letters are frequently written on the company’s letterhead.

E. Sponsorship Letter

    A sponsorship letter is a document that is written to request financial support or sponsorship from an individual, company, or organization. It is a formal letter that outlines the purpose of the sponsorship and the benefits that will be received by the sponsor. The letter should be concise, clear, and professional in tone. It is essential to make sure that the letter is written with the sponsor's interests in mind and shows how the sponsorship will benefit both parties.

    The sponsorship letter should include the details of the event or project that needs sponsorship, the amount of money required, and the benefits that the sponsor will receive in return for the support. This may include advertising opportunities, branding exposure, and publicity. The sponsorship letter should also provide information about the organization or individual requesting sponsorship, including its mission, values, and goals.

4 Types of Sponsorship Letters

    Event Sponsorship Letter: This type of letter is written to request sponsorship for a specific event, such as a fundraiser or conference. The letter should include details about the event, including the date, location, and expected attendance, as well as the benefits that the sponsor will receive in return for their support.

    Sports Sponsorship Letter: This type of letter is written to request sponsorship for a sports team or athlete. The letter should include details about the team or athlete, including their achievements and upcoming events, as well as the benefits that the sponsor will receive in return for their support.

    Education Sponsorship Letter: This type of letter is written to request sponsorship for educational programs or initiatives, such as scholarships or school programs. The letter should include details about the program or initiative, as well as the benefits that the sponsor will receive in return for their support.

    Nonprofit Sponsorship Letter: This type of letter is written to request sponsorship for a nonprofit organization. The letter should include details about the organization

EMAIL ETIQUETTE WRITING

Rules for email etiquette:

1. Use a clear, professional subject line

    Show your recipient clearly what the email will cover. Many people will decide whether they will open an email depending on the subject line. For someone who gets hundreds of emails a day, a subject line that is to the point makes it easier for them to sort through their inbox and decide what communications to prioritize.

2. Proofread every email you send

    Make sure there are no grammatical or professional errors. Have you spelt the recipient’s name correctly? Are there spelling errors? Are you using simple sentence structures and correct capitalisation and punctuation? Ignoring these compromises your professionalism and the credibility of your email.

3. Write your email before entering the recipient email address

    It is always best practice to write the contents of your email first in case you accidentally send the message too early.

4. Double check you have the correct recipient

    There is nothing worse than sending an email to the wrong Jess or a confidential document to the wrong client or company.

5. Ensure you CC all relevant recipients

    It is unprofessional to leave out a colleague or client from a relevant email chain. Be mindful of who should be informed about a given matter and respect that.

6. You don't always have to "reply all"

    Think about who needs to read your response; no one wants to read an email chain from 20 people that has nothing to do with them.

7. Reply to your emails

    Most people at some point have felt swamped by the large number of emails they have to sift through. But replying to an email is good etiquette, especially if the sender is expecting a response. Acknowledging you received the email but will get back to the sender at a later time is a professional alternative to ignoring or avoiding certain emails.

8. Include a signature block

    If your recipient doesn’t know anything about you, they may be skeptical of the authenticity of your email. It is professional to include your full name, title, your company and your contact number.

9. Use the appropriate level of formality

    For instance, begin with “Dear _____”, use “please” and “thank you” where necessary, and always end your email with the appropriate phrase, “Kind regards”, “Thank you”, “Sincerely” and so on.

10. Keep emails brief and to the point

    No one wants to read an enormous chunk of text. You can always follow up on the matter later or suggest they give you a call if they have any queries or concerns.
